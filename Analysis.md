# Analysis: System Archetypes, Scenarios, and Leverage Points

**BSAD 482 Term Project — Milestone 3 (Path A: Systems Focus)**
**Decision:** Should a first-time homebuyer in the Halifax Regional Municipality buy a home in 2026 or wait approximately 18 months?

---

## 1. System Archetype Identification

### Chosen Archetype: Growth and Underinvestment

The **Growth and Underinvestment** archetype describes a situation where a system is growing toward a limit, but that limit could be raised through timely investment. Instead, investment is delayed or underfunded, so the system stalls — or conditions worsen — before supply catches up to demand.

The generic structure looks like this:

- Growing demand pushes performance toward a capacity limit
- The limit creates pressure to invest in new capacity
- But investment is delayed (due to cost, time, policy, or political will)
- In the meantime, demand continues to grow and conditions deteriorate
- Eventually capacity catches up, but the gap causes real harm in the interim

This archetype is distinct from *Limits to Growth* because the limit is not fixed — it *can* be raised, and that is precisely what makes the delay so consequential.

### Mapping to the Halifax Housing Context

Halifax's housing system fits this archetype closely across several dimensions.

**The growth:** Halifax's population grew from approximately 414,000 in 2015 to 568,000 by 2025 — a 37% increase driven by interprovincial migration, international immigration, and post-secondary student retention. This population growth continuously expanded the demand side of the housing market.

**The limit:** Physical housing supply — the number of available units to rent or purchase — is the binding constraint. Supply cannot respond instantly to demand; new construction takes 2–5 years from rezoning to occupancy.

**The delayed investment:** Housing starts in Halifax rose from approximately 2,850 units per year in 2015 to 5,400 by 2025, a meaningful acceleration, but consistently lagged behind what population growth required to maintain affordability. CMHC has estimated that Canada needs to nearly double its construction pace to restore affordability by 2030 (Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation, 2023). In Halifax specifically, zoning restrictions, permitting timelines, and construction labour shortages all contributed to the investment delay.

**The consequence:** Because supply investment was delayed, the gap between demand and available housing caused prices to rise sharply — particularly from 2020 to 2022 — and rental vacancy rates to fall to historic lows of 0.8% in 2022. By the time housing starts accelerated in 2023–2025, affordability had already deteriorated significantly. The months-of-inventory measure, which represents the supply side of market conditions, only began recovering toward a balanced level (~3 months) in 2024, years after demand first accelerated.

**Key variables and loops involved:**

The archetype operates primarily through the **B3 Supply Response Loop** from the refined CLD: rising housing prices (+) signal developers to increase supply (+), which adds to inventory and increases days on market (+), which moderates prices (−). However, the time delay built into this loop — the gap between a price signal and actual new units being available — is what creates the Growth and Underinvestment dynamic. The reinforcing **R1 Demand-Confidence Loop** (demand → prices → confidence → demand) accelerates during the delay period, pushing prices even higher before the supply response arrives.

**Evidence from the data:**

Figure 4 (Visualization 4, Milestone 2) shows this pattern directly. Population grew steeply and continuously from 2015 onward, while housing starts responded with a noticeable lag — starts remained relatively flat from 2015 to 2020 before accelerating after 2021. The rental vacancy rate, which fell from 3.5% in 2015 to 0.8% in 2022, is a direct indicator of the supply gap. The price surge shown in Figure 1 — average sale prices nearly doubling between 2019 and 2022 — is the classic consequence of demand outrunning a constrained, slow-to-respond supply. The partial inventory recovery visible in Figure 2 (months of supply rising from ~2 to ~3.5 from 2022 to 2025) reflects the delayed investment finally arriving, but it has not yet restored affordability to pre-shortage levels.

A secondary archetype also operating in this context is **Shifting the Burden**: individual buyers and renters cope with the housing shortage through short-term fixes (accepting higher rents, moving further from the city, delaying purchase) rather than addressing the fundamental supply problem. These coping strategies reduce the immediate pressure to solve the structural issue and allow policymakers to delay the harder work of meaningful supply-side reform. Over time, this entrenches the affordability problem.

## 2. Scenario Narratives

### Scenario 1: Status Quo — The Buyer Continues to Wait

In this scenario, the first-time homebuyer decides not to purchase in 2026 and continues renting while monitoring the market, hoping that prices will fall or that affordability will improve meaningfully before they commit.

Over the next five to ten years, the system continues to operate largely as it has since 2023. The Bank of Canada holds rates in the 2.25–3.00% range, keeping five-year posted mortgage rates between 4.5% and 5.5%. Halifax home prices, supported by persistent population growth and constrained supply, appreciate at approximately 3–4% annually. By 2028, the average Halifax home that costs $530,000 today would be priced at roughly $570,000–$590,000. The buyer's monthly rent, already at $1,790 in 2025, continues rising at 4–5% per year as the rental vacancy rate remains tight — reaching approximately $2,100–$2,200 by 2028.

The **B1 Affordability Balancing Loop** does provide some relief as rates continue to ease, but this is partially offset by the **R1 Demand-Confidence Loop** re-activating: lower rates bring more buyers back into the market, sustaining price growth. The **B3 Supply Response Loop** is working — new construction is accelerating — but the three-to-five year lag between a start and occupancy means the supply addition does not translate into meaningfully lower prices within the buyer's wait window.

The key uncertainty in this scenario is whether a significant economic shock — a recession, a sharp rise in unemployment, or a policy-driven immigration reduction — interrupts demand. If such a shock occurs, prices could correct 10–15%, creating a genuine buying opportunity. However, this is unpredictable, and waiting indefinitely for a shock that may not materialize carries its own cost: the buyer accumulates approximately $32,000–$40,000 in rent payments per 18-month wait period, none of which builds equity. The most likely unintended consequence of this scenario is that the buyer is permanently priced out as a combination of price appreciation and life changes (changing income, family circumstances) narrows the window further.

---

### Scenario 2: Intervention A — The Buyer Purchases in 2026

In this scenario, the first-time homebuyer decides to purchase a home in 2026 at or near current market conditions — approximately $530,000 with a 10% down payment and a five-year fixed mortgage at the current posted rate of approximately 4.54%.

Over the following five to ten years, the buyer benefits from several favourable dynamics. First, the **B3 Supply Response Loop** continues to add housing stock, which moderates price growth — but because the buyer has locked in a purchase price today, they benefit from any appreciation that does occur while being insulated from further price increases. If Halifax prices appreciate at 3% annually, the $530,000 home is worth approximately $615,000 by 2031, representing roughly $85,000 in paper equity gains in addition to the principal paid down over five years of mortgage payments.

Second, the buyer exits the rental market, which disconnects them from the **B2 Rental Pressure Loop**. With rental vacancy rates stubbornly below 2%, rents are likely to continue rising for the foreseeable future — the buyer avoids this escalating cost entirely. Third, because the Bank of Canada is in an easing cycle, there is a reasonable probability that the buyer's next mortgage renewal (at the five-year mark, around 2031) occurs at a lower rate than today's 4.54%, reducing monthly payments at renewal.

The primary risk in this scenario is that prices decline after purchase. A 10–15% correction — plausible if a recession occurs or if the federal government significantly curtails immigration — would reduce the home's value to approximately $450,000–$475,000, putting the buyer in a negative equity position relative to their down payment in the short term. A second uncertainty is employment stability: buying at a stretched affordability ratio (~43% of income) leaves limited financial buffer if income falls. The key unintended consequence to watch is the risk of becoming "house poor" — owning the home but having insufficient income left over to maintain quality of life or build other savings.

---

### Scenario 3: Intervention B — The Buyer Waits Strategically for 12 Months

In this scenario, the buyer does not purchase immediately but sets a firm 12-month window — targeting a purchase in early to mid-2027 — and uses the time purposefully: increasing their down payment savings, monitoring the market, and waiting to see whether the Bank of Canada's easing cycle produces a further reduction in five-year mortgage rates.

Over the 12-month wait, the buyer pays approximately $21,500 in rent. If the BoC continues cutting and the five-year posted rate falls from 4.54% to approximately 4.0–4.25% by early 2027 — a plausible outcome given the current easing trajectory — the monthly mortgage payment on a $545,000 home (after ~3% price appreciation) drops from approximately $2,630 to approximately $2,480–$2,530. This is a modest but real improvement. The buyer also has 12 additional months to build savings, potentially increasing the down payment from 10% to 12–13%, which reduces the principal and eliminates the CMHC mortgage insurance premium that applies to down payments below 20%.

This scenario operates most effectively if the **B3 Supply Response Loop** continues to add inventory, keeping months of supply near 3–3.5 months and preventing a return to the 2021-era seller's market. The **R1 Demand-Confidence Loop** is the key risk: if lower rates bring a surge of buyers back before the buyer is ready to act, competition could push prices up faster than the rate savings offset. The 2020 experience — where rates dropped and prices surged within months — is a real precedent.

The main unintended consequence here is indecision extending beyond 12 months. Scenario 3 is disciplined by design: it requires the buyer to commit to a specific timeline rather than waiting indefinitely. If the buyer continues to delay past mid-2027 "to see what happens," Scenario 3 collapses back into Scenario 1, with all of the associated costs.

## 3. Leverage Point Analysis

### Identified Leverage Point: Housing Supply Capacity (Speed and Volume of New Construction)

Of all the variables in the refined CLD, the most promising leverage point for improving outcomes in the Halifax housing system is the rate at which new housing supply is built and brought to market — specifically, the speed and volume of residential construction.

**Why this point offers high impact relative to effort:**

Supply is the variable that affects the most feedback loops simultaneously. Increasing the pace and volume of construction directly:

- Strengthens the **B3 Supply Response Loop** by shortening the delay between price signals and new units reaching buyers, dampening the price surges that occur when demand outpaces supply
- Weakens the **R1 Demand-Confidence Loop** by reducing scarcity-driven buyer urgency and speculative price expectations
- Eases the **B2 Rental Pressure Loop** by increasing the rental stock available, which supports vacancy rate recovery and moderates rent increases
- Reduces the severity of the **Growth and Underinvestment** archetype by closing the gap between population growth and housing capacity

In practical terms, every percentage point increase in housing supply growth relative to population growth reduces the structural upward pressure on prices. CMHC's 2023 Housing Supply Report estimated that Canada requires approximately 3.5 million additional units above the baseline construction trajectory to restore affordability by 2030. Halifax's proportional contribution to that gap is meaningful given the city's above-average population growth rate.

The specific interventions at this leverage point include accelerated permitting timelines, zoning reform to allow missing-middle and higher-density housing in established neighbourhoods, density bonuses for affordable or rental units, and investment in construction workforce capacity.

**What feedback loops it affects:**

As noted above, this leverage point directly affects B3, and indirectly dampens R1 and B2. It does not affect the mortgage rate chain (BoC policy rate → mortgage rate → affordability) at all — that is a separate lever controlled by the Bank of Canada. This is important: supply and monetary policy operate as complementary interventions, not substitutes. Rate cuts alone, without supply growth, tend to re-activate R1 by releasing pent-up demand into a still-constrained market, producing another price surge rather than an affordability improvement.

**Risks and resistance:**

The main risk of intervening at this leverage point is implementation lag. Even with accelerated permitting and zoning reform, new construction takes two to five years to reach occupancy. This means supply-side interventions taken today do not improve affordability for today's buyer — they improve conditions for buyers in 2028 and beyond. For the individual first-time buyer making a decision in 2026, this leverage point is not directly actionable; it is a systemic improvement that benefits future buyers more than present ones.

Resistance to supply-side intervention is well-documented. Existing homeowners often oppose densification in their neighbourhoods because it threatens property values and neighbourhood character. Municipal governments face political pressure from this constituency and may move slowly on zoning reform even when the affordability case is clear. These structural resistances are part of why the Growth and Underinvestment archetype persists — the actors who could fund the investment face incentives that discourage them from acting quickly.

## 4. Implications for the Decision

The systems analysis conducted in this milestone clarifies the decision facing a Halifax first-time homebuyer in 2026 in several important ways.

The dominant pattern in the Halifax housing system is Growth and Underinvestment: population demand has consistently outpaced housing supply, and the delays built into the supply response mean that this gap closes slowly. This structural reality means that waiting for prices to fall significantly is unlikely to be a rewarding strategy in the absence of an external shock. The supply-side investment is now accelerating, but its effects will take years to fully materialize — and in the meantime, the R1 Demand-Confidence Loop is prone to re-activation each time interest rates fall and buyer confidence returns.

The scenario analysis suggests that the buyer's most consequential variable is not the timing of the purchase but the interest rate environment at the time of purchase. The difference between buying at 4.54% versus 4.0% is approximately $150/month on a $530,000 mortgage — meaningful but not decisive. The difference between buying now and renting for 18 more months, by contrast, involves approximately $32,000 in rent payments that build no equity, plus continued exposure to rental cost escalation through the B2 loop.

The leverage point analysis confirms that the structural affordability problem in Halifax is a supply-side issue that no individual buyer can solve. The practical implication is that the buyer should not wait for the system to fix itself on a timeline that suits their circumstances.

Key uncertainties that remain include: the path of the Bank of Canada policy rate over the next 12–18 months; the possibility of a demand-reducing event such as a federal immigration policy shift or economic recession; and the degree to which accelerated housing starts in 2024–2025 translate into actual completions and meaningful inventory relief by 2027.

On balance, the analysis points toward the conclusion that purchasing in 2026 — or within a firm 12-month window — is the more defensible option for a buyer with stable employment and adequate savings. Waiting indefinitely exposes the buyer to the compounding costs of the B2 Rental Pressure Loop without a credible mechanism for prices to fall significantly enough to justify the delay. A more detailed recommendation, including sensitivity to the buyer's specific financial situation, will be developed in Milestone 4.
